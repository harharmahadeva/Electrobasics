LESSON PURPOSE
============================================================

This lesson introduces diodes as electronic components that allow current to flow mainly in one direction.
Beginners will learn what a diode looks like, why polarity matters, how to identify anode and cathode, and where diodes are used in real circuits.

============================================================
BEGINNER-FRIENDLY THEORY | शुरुआती सिद्धांत
============================================================

English

A diode is a two-terminal electronic component.
It has two sides:

• Anode
• Cathode

A diode allows current to flow easily from anode to cathode when it is forward biased.
It blocks current when reverse biased.

Simple idea:
A diode is like a one-way valve for electric current.

------------------------------------------------------------
DIODE TERMINALS
------------------------------------------------------------

Anode:
The positive side for normal forward current.

Cathode:
The negative side for normal forward current.
On many real diodes, the cathode side is marked with a stripe or band.

Current direction in basic diagrams:
Conventional current flows from anode to cathode when diode is forward biased.

------------------------------------------------------------
FORWARD BIAS
------------------------------------------------------------

Forward bias means the diode is connected in the direction that allows current.

Condition:
Anode is connected to higher voltage.
Cathode is connected to lower voltage.

In this condition, the diode conducts current.

A silicon diode usually needs about 0.6V to 0.7V forward voltage before it conducts strongly.

Example:
If the multimeter diode test shows 0.62V, the diode is likely working in forward direction.

------------------------------------------------------------
REVERSE BIAS
------------------------------------------------------------

Reverse bias means the diode is connected in the direction that blocks current.

Condition:
Cathode is connected to higher voltage.
Anode is connected to lower voltage.

In normal beginner circuits, very little current flows in reverse bias.

If reverse voltage becomes too high, a normal diode can break down and become damaged.

------------------------------------------------------------
DIODE SYMBOL
------------------------------------------------------------

The diode symbol looks like an arrow/triangle pointing toward a line.

Anode side → Cathode line

The vertical line in the symbol represents the cathode.
This matches the stripe on many real diodes.

Beginner memory:
Line on symbol = cathode.
Stripe on real diode = cathode.

------------------------------------------------------------
FORWARD VOLTAGE DROP
------------------------------------------------------------

A diode is not a perfect wire.
When current flows through it, it drops some voltage.

Typical approximate values:
• Silicon diode: about 0.6V to 0.7V
• Schottky diode: about 0.2V to 0.4V
• LED: depends on color, often about 1.8V to 3.3V

Beginner note:
LEDs are also diodes, but they emit light. LEDs have their own lesson next.

------------------------------------------------------------
COMMON DIODE TYPES
------------------------------------------------------------

1. Rectifier Diode
Used to convert AC to DC and handle power current.
Example: 1N4007.

2. Signal Diode
Used in small signal circuits.
Example: 1N4148.

3. Schottky Diode
Fast diode with lower voltage drop.
Used in power and switching circuits.

4. Zener Diode
Designed to work in reverse breakdown at a specific voltage.
Used for voltage reference and protection.

5. LED
Light Emitting Diode.
Emits light when forward biased.

6. Photodiode
Responds to light.
Used in sensors and optical communication.

7. TVS Diode
Used for surge and ESD protection.

This beginner lesson focuses mainly on ordinary diodes, rectifier diodes and signal diodes.
LEDs and Zener diodes will be explored separately in later lessons.

------------------------------------------------------------
WHY DIODES ARE USEFUL
------------------------------------------------------------

Diodes are used for:

• Reverse polarity protection
• AC to DC rectification
• Flyback protection across relays and motors
• Signal steering
• Voltage clamping
• Power OR-ing
• Surge protection
• Light emission in LEDs

------------------------------------------------------------
DIODE TEST WITH MULTIMETER
------------------------------------------------------------

Most digital multimeters have diode test mode.

To test a normal silicon diode:

Forward direction:
Red probe to anode.
Black probe to cathode.
Meter may show around 0.6V to 0.7V.

Reverse direction:
Red probe to cathode.
Black probe to anode.
Meter should show OL or no conduction.

If both directions show 0V or very low reading:
Diode may be shorted.

If both directions show OL:
Diode may be open or not connected properly.

ANALOGY | तुलना
============================================================

Water Valve Analogy

Water pipe = wire
Water flow = electric current
One-way valve = diode

A one-way valve allows water to flow in one direction.
If water tries to flow backward, the valve blocks it.

In electronics:
A diode allows current mainly in one direction.
It blocks current in the opposite direction.

Important:
This analogy helps beginners understand direction, but a diode also has voltage drop and electrical limits.

============================================================
STEP-BY-STEP: IDENTIFYING A DIODE
============================================================

Step 1:
Look at the body shape.

Small glass body:
Often signal diode such as 1N4148.

Black cylindrical body with stripe:
Often rectifier diode such as 1N4007.

Small black SMD package:
Could be many diode types.

Step 2:
Find the stripe.

The stripe usually marks the cathode.

Step 3:
Compare with symbol.

Cathode stripe on real diode matches the line in the schematic symbol.

Step 4:
Read the part number if printed.

Examples:
1N4007 = rectifier diode
1N4148 = signal diode
SS14 = Schottky diode

Step 5:
Verify with multimeter diode test mode.

============================================================
FORWARD VS REVERSE BIAS TABLE
============================================================

Forward Bias:
Anode higher, cathode lower.
Diode conducts.
Meter shows forward voltage.
Used when we want current to pass.

Reverse Bias:
Cathode higher, anode lower.
Diode blocks.
Meter shows OL.
Used when we want current blocked or protected.

COMMON BEGINNER DIODE PARTS
============================================================

1N4007:
Common rectifier diode.
Used in power and protection circuits.
Handles higher reverse voltage than many small signal diodes.

1N4148:
Small signal diode.
Used for switching and signal circuits.
Fast and common in beginner kits.

1N5819:
Schottky diode.
Lower forward voltage.
Useful in low-voltage circuits.

Zener Diode:
Used in reverse direction at a rated voltage.
Beginners should not treat it like a normal diode without understanding.

LED:
A diode that emits light.
Needs current-limiting resistor.
Covered in next lesson.

PRACTICAL LAB | प्रैक्टिकल लैब
============================================================

Lab Name:
Test a Diode with a Digital Multimeter

Required Components:
• 1N4007 diode or similar rectifier diode
• 1N4148 diode if available
• Digital multimeter with diode test mode
• Breadboard
• LED and resistor optional
• Notebook

Safety:
• Use low-voltage components only.
• Do not test diodes inside powered circuits.
• Power OFF before measuring components.
• Do not use mains power.

Part A: Identify Diode Direction

Steps:
1. Pick a diode.
2. Find the stripe on one side.
3. Mark that side as cathode.
4. The other side is anode.
5. Compare with the diode symbol.

Part B: Multimeter Diode Test

Steps:
1. Set multimeter to diode test mode.
2. Connect red probe to anode.
3. Connect black probe to cathode.
4. Record the reading.
5. Reverse probes.
6. Record the reading again.

Expected Result:
Forward direction:
About 0.6V to 0.7V for silicon diode.

Reverse direction:
OL or no reading.

Observation Table:

Diode Part | Stripe Side Identified? | Forward Reading | Reverse Reading | Result
1N4007     |                         |                 |                |
1N4148     |                         |                 |                |
Schottky   |                         |                 |                |

Part C: Simple Direction Test with LED

Circuit:
Battery + → resistor → diode → LED → battery -

Try diode in one direction.
Then flip it.
Observe when LED lights.

Important:
Always include a resistor with LED.

============================================================
COMMON BEGINNER MISTAKES | सामान्य शुरुआती गलतियाँ
============================================================

• Thinking diode works both ways like a resistor.
• Ignoring the stripe on the diode body.
• Confusing anode and cathode.
• Forgetting that diode has voltage drop.
• Testing a diode while the circuit is powered.
• Using diode test mode but touching probes incorrectly.
• Assuming every black cylindrical part is the same diode.
• Using a small signal diode where a power diode is needed.
• Connecting LED without a resistor.
• Thinking OL always means broken diode; in reverse direction OL can be normal.

============================================================
TROUBLESHOOTING GUIDE | समस्या समाधान
============================================================

Problem:
Diode test shows OL in both directions.

Possible Causes:
• Diode is open/faulty.
• Probe contact is poor.
• Wrong meter mode.
• Diode leads are dirty/oxidized.

Problem:
Diode test shows 0V or very low reading both directions.

Possible Cause:
• Diode may be shorted.

Problem:
Circuit does not work with diode.

Check:
• Is diode direction correct?
• Is the cathode stripe on the correct side?
• Is the voltage high enough to overcome diode drop?
• Is there a resistor where needed?
• Are wires in correct breadboard rows?

Problem:
LED does not light after adding diode.

Possible Causes:
• Diode reversed.
• LED reversed.
• Resistor too high.
• Battery voltage too low.
• Breadboard wiring wrong.

Problem:
Diode gets hot.

Possible Causes:
• Too much current.
• Wrong diode rating.
• Short circuit.
• Incorrect wiring.

Action:
Turn power OFF immediately and inspect.

============================================================
SAFETY SECTION | सुरक्षा
============================================================

Diodes are usually safe in low-voltage beginner circuits, but unsafe wiring can still cause heat or damage.

Important Rules:
• Use current-limiting resistor with LEDs.
• Do not connect diode directly across a battery without understanding the circuit.
• Check current rating of the diode.
• Do not test components in powered circuits.
• Do not work on mains-powered chargers or power supplies as a beginner.
• If a diode gets hot, power off.

Hindi:

QUIZ | प्रश्नोत्तरी
============================================================

Q1. A diode mainly allows current in how many directions?
Answer:
One direction.

Q2. What are the two terminals of a diode?
Answer:
Anode and cathode.

Q3. What usually marks the cathode side on a real diode?
Answer:
A stripe or band.

Q4. In forward bias, does a diode conduct?
Answer:
Yes.

Q5. In reverse bias, does a normal diode block current?
Answer:
Yes, in normal conditions.

Q6. What is the typical forward voltage of a silicon diode?
Answer:
About 0.6V to 0.7V.

Q7. What does OL usually mean during reverse diode test?
Answer:
No conduction / open reading, which is normal in reverse direction.

Q8. Is LED a type of diode?
Answer:
Yes.

Q9. Should a diode be tested inside a powered circuit?
Answer:
No.

Q10. What diode is commonly used as a rectifier diode?
Answer:
1N4007 or similar.
